## Post 7 (AI)
The honest version of "AI automation" — and why most agencies over-promise it.

A real automation project = 4 things:
1. The LLM call (cheap, fast, impressive in screenshots).
2. The data plumbing (slow, fragile, ugly).
3. The error handling (the work that gets quoted wrong).
4. The audit trail (the work clients actually care about).

Most agencies quote (1) and bolt (2)-(4) at extra cost. Most clients buy (1) and forget about (2)-(4).

The companies winning at this are quietly absorbing (3) and (4) into their pricing so it doesn't surprise the client mid-project.

We do the same at Opus.

## Post 10 (AI)
I'm running Opus's sales pipeline with 70% AI-generated outreach.

The pipeline I built:
– Scrape: towns × business category → ~120 handles/run
– Score: reply rate from previous campaigns → 0-100 score
– Generate: each score gets a personalized first-touch using a different LLM prompt template
– Send: scheduled across 7 days so I'm not a 4am mass-blast account

Result: 23% open, 4.2% reply, 1.8% booked-call, on a 100-business run. Last manual month was 18%, 2%, 0.4% on the same pool.

AI accelerated the boring part. The human closes it.

## Post 15 (AI) 🔥 hot take
If your "AI automation" only works at the demo stage, it's not automation. It's a magic trick.

A real automation is one that survives:
– A weekend where you're not checking on it.
– A client changing their form field names.
– An API rate limit you didn't know existed.

My checklist before declaring a workflow "automated":
– Blindfolded demo for a non-engineer.
– 7 days in production without my intervention.
– Edge cases (what happens on cell network drops, what happens on bad input).
– Logging the things I'd be paged on if I were a dev on call.

If even one fails, it's "in development" — not automated.

## Post 27 (Hot Take)
Why I think every ECE UG should run a side hustle in college:

Not for the money. Not for the resume (well, partly for the resume). For the shortage of adult-problems solved before graduation.

Real client work forces you to:
– Define a problem someone else owns
– Scope it
– Price it
– Deliver under deadline
– Handle scope creep
– Ask for the next project

These are not taught well in 4th sem.

If you're not doing real client work yet, find one by 5th sem or you'll graduate having only solved homework problems.

## Post 28 (Lessons)
Three months ago I thought I knew how to run a video agency.

Things I now know I didn't know:
1. The kind of client who says "we want something cool" almost never means what they say. Always ask for 3 reference reels in their category.
2. Reels that test well on internal team are not the reels that test well on Instagram. Real engagement is its own audience feedback loop.
3. Endless revisions on the first reel = client signal that will repeat forever. Lock revisions in your contract.

Each came from a real client. None from a YouTube tutorial.

## Post 29 (D) 🔥 hot take
"Build a brand for Indian SMBs" sounds noble in a tagline.

What's actually true:
– The owner of the local sweet mart doesn't care about brand identity.
– They care about orders next Diwali.
– Anything we've built that worked has been tied to a specific order-driving action.
– Anything that *looked* beautiful but didn't drive orders got quietly archived.

This is why I have no patience for "abstract brand designers."

Every visual asset Opus ships has an order-driving hypothesis attached. Sometimes the hypothesis is wrong. We re-shoot.

If your brand work isn't measurable in sales, it isn't brand work. It's expensive art.
