## 2. THREE ANGLES BY AUDIENCE

### A. RECRUITER ANGLE — "ECE student who already runs a real agency"

The point is to demystify his competence. Recruiters want to know: *will this person ship? Will this person manage? Is this person's engineering foundation real?*

Tone: calm, competence-laden, no inflation. Aim is to be the obvious answer to "where's the most talented ECE UG applying today."

Lead elements to include:
- ECE specific coursework (signals/dsp/embedded systems = the AI/robotics side)
- Real shipped product (Dayo)
- Real shipped service business (Opus with named clients)
- Real motion-design portfolio (the rare dual-competence that flips the script from "another dev")

Avoid: "aspiring AI engineer," "passionate builder," "looking for opportunities."

Suggested opener for recruiter outreach (DM or LinkedIn cover):
> *"I run an AI automation studio out of my 2nd year of ECE at GHRCEMN. Dayo (a PWA for Indian engineering students) is what I build between client projects. If you're hiring for engineering internships this fall, I'd like 15 minutes."*

This works because:
- Specific college name = credible
- Specific product = proof of capacity
- Self-aware "between client projects" = signals not desperation
- "15 minutes" = low-friction ask

### B. FOUNDER ANGLE — "Student who solved his own problem and is shipping to others"

Audience: angel investors / potential cofounders / agency clients. Goal: prove pattern recognition + execution.

Tone: student who started building because he needed a fix, kept going because others needed it.

This works because:
- Names a peer-familiar brand (Namma Yedi)
- Quotes concrete numbers (a month, five clients)

### C. STUDENT ANGLE — "Peer who figured something out and is sharing it"

Audience: other engineering students — the same ones who'll install Dayo. This audience is the user-acquisition loop, not the consulting loop.

Tone: friendly, slightly amused, no condescension. Siddhant should sound like the smart senior in a study group, not a brand.

Lead elements:
- The motion-design tutorials
- The "here's how I built X during exams" threads
- The Dayo behind-the-scenes
- The Quick Tips from his work at Opus

**Voice sample:**
> *"Bunked my way to 73% in 4th sem. Spent the next 6 weeks building an app to track it. Now the rest of my batch is using it. Here's how I built it."*

This works because:
- Humor + honesty
- Actionable thread ready
- The "now my batch is using it" line is free distribution

## 4. WHAT NOT TO SAY — the LinkedIn cliches that will quietly destroy his positioning

These are the lines that signal "any other 2nd-year ECE student desperate for an internship."

1. **"Passionate about AI and machine learning. Looking for opportunities."**
   Every tech-bro LinkedIn bio starts here. Recruiters physically scroll past.

2. **"Excited to share that I just completed X."**
   The "excited to share" frame positions him as a fan of his own work. Don't.

3. **"I am a self-motivated, hard-working, team player…"**
   All-soft-skills. Reads like a resume template. Replace with shipped work.

4. **"Currently exploring opportunities in… companies / startups / etc."**
   Generic and passive. Either ask for the specific thing or don't post.

5. **"Blessed/grateful to announce…"**
   Indian LinkedIn cliché. SaaS-style announcement on Instagram still happens but it dates you.

6. **"Through this experience I learned the value of… and… the importance of…"**
   This is an English-class lesson, not a build-in-public post. Replace with a concrete number or a concrete mistake.

7. **"DM me if you want to collab/use my product/learn more."**
   Make the post have a CTA from a position of confidence, not a request from one of need.

8. **"Just shipped / Built this weekend / Side project Friday."**
   Doesn't say what the thing does. Always restate the *thing*, then the meta ("It does X. I built it in Y.")

(For Twitter, the analogous cliches — "Grateful," "Blessed," "Just posted ⬇️" — are similarly destructive.)

## 5. TONE ANCHORS — for any future copy

When Siddhant's voice starts drifting, return to these:

1. **Honest before polished.**
   A post that's a true admission of failure beats a glossy release.

2. **Numbers, not adjectives.**
   "5 paying clients" beats "successful agency." "3 crashes in week 1" beats "early learnings."

3. **Indian English is fine.**
   "Bunk," "class," "midsem," "cram," "ek minute," "proper solid" — all usable. Audience is Indian.

4. **Specificity over universality.**
   "I almost got detained" beats "Learning the importance of discipline."

5. **Always leave the hook dangling.**
   The end of a post is where the conversion happens. End on a number, a question, or a dogmatic take. Don't summarize.
