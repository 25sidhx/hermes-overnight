# Research — Indian Students Who Built In Public

Sources: X/Twitter posts, LinkedIn profiles, Instagram posts, YouTube interviews, public blogs. Profiles drawn from publicly indexed content at time of search (June 2026). Where my actual engagement with the account is limited, I've marked inferences as such.

The goal is not "Indian student success porn." It's: what content formats + positioning actually drove followings? What broke early and is recoverable? What mistakes get repeated?

---

## 1. Varun Mayya (@waitin4agi_ / @thevarunmayya)

**Personal snapshot:** Entrepreneur, founder of Aeos Labs. AI-driven content creation company. Age ~30 now. Active on X and Instagram.

**What he built:** Originally a consumer app (Globally), pivoted into AI-content tooling through Aeos. Built a publicly visible AI clone that posts content for him.

**How he positioned himself online:** "I help entrepreneurs build software, content, small companies, and freelancing careers." Owner of the "Indian AI grifter / tech-bro done well" lane. Bio: "I am an idiot" — skepticism-coded signal.

**Content style:**
- Short, opinionated, fast takes. Often single screenshots (text from his phone, news headlines, Twitter screenshots).
- Meme-heavy when relevant. Sarcasm + nuance combo.
- High-frequency on Instagram (multiple posts/week), more selective on X.
- Recurring angles: AI hype, AI ethics, Bangalore startup culture, the grift economy, physics envy ("the way physics is taught in colleges makes me sad"), autodidactic systems (he gets AI agents to install Node.js on his machine).

**What works for him:**
- His tone — confident + self-aware. "I am an idiot" lowers critic shields.
- Cross-posting between Instagram and Twitter makes his material feel like a system, not accounts.
- Riding on real news and trending moments, never generic.
- Explicit effort to share his system, not just results — encourages followers to copy what he's doing.

**Mistakes he made:**
- Multiple personae that occasionally contradict (early GlobGlobally positioning vs AI philosophical posts).
- Some folks now consider his AI clone a stunt vs substance. Identity is fragile when the system is the brand, not the person.

**What Siddhant can take:** the high-frequency opinionated take rhythm. The use of screenshots of his phone (low effort, high authenticity). The self-aware tone. NOT the volume — Varun treats content as full-time work.

## 2. Tanay Pratap (@tanaypratap)

**Personal snapshot:** Senior Software Engineer at Microsoft when I last confirmed; mentor to Indian students; YouTube presence at scale (hundreds of thousands).

**What he built:** Career-progression story: classroom teacher → Microsoft → indie creator → mentor figure. IndiaSpend digital, then Microsoft via the engineer route, then turned his growth playbook into a public persona.

**Positioning:** "Engineer first, teacher second, content creator third." Deduced voice: empathetic, narrative-first, almost always describing student struggles before pivoting to lessons.

**Content style:**
- Long-form: essays on Twitter (~700-1000 chars), threads that read more like blog posts than hot takes.
- Book recommendations + life principles + workplace stories.
- Fewer daily posts than Varun; every post is a "moment."
- Heavy use of student-journey framing ("I was in the same boat three years ago").

**What works:**
- Relatability for Tier-2 college students specifically.
- Hindi + English mix on YouTube, English on Twitter.
- He engages with his own past posts via tweets, building themes.
- The lesson-tied-to-personal-failure structure is gold.

**Mistakes:**
- "Mentor" framing can feel preachy if not balanced with his own stumbles.
- Slower growth on new platforms vs Twitter because the lesson format doesn't translate.

**Take for Siddhant:** Use the lesson-before-pivot story arc. Reference personal failure before any claim. Lean into "engineering student with hands dirty on shipped work" voice.

## 3. Akshay Pachaar (@akshay_pachaar)

**Personal snapshot:** AI engineer + content creator. Active on LinkedIn and Twitter. Less famous than Tanay but a working example of the AI-engineer-as-creator lane.

**What he built:** Multi-agent content systems using Motia (per his LinkedIn post). Builds agentic workflows with LangChain, vector stores, RAG systems.

**Positioning:** AI engineer who shares his own stack. "I use the thing, then I write about it." Practitioner-first.

**Content style:**
- Heavy on technical breakdowns.
- Posts are essentially tutorials disguised as LinkedIn "lessons."
- Visuals are mostly code/architecture diagrams.

**What works:**
- Practitioner-trust signals (he shows the actual code).
- Spans HIS tool stack + what he's building; doesn't chase trends cheap.
- The engineer-as-creator lane is wide open in India.

**Mistakes:**
- Technical depth = lower viral ceiling. Slower growth than opinion-driven creators.
- Vulnerable to looking dated as AI tech shifts.

**Take:** Siddhant's "I'm an ECE student *running* an AI automation agency" perfectly fits this lane but with the undergrad angle = viral upside Tanay and Akshay don't have.

## 4. Shubham V. / Dyn8n (LinkedIn: in.linkedin.com/in/shubhamvs)

**Personal snapshot:** 19-year-old full-stack engineer (per his current bio) building Dyn8n. Six-time national hackathon winner.

**What he built:** Dyn8n (a workflow automation tool, name similar to n8n). Self-taught full stack from the MERN era.

**Positioning:** "Six-time national hackathon winner / AI builder." Self-taught as authenticity signal.

**Content style:**
- Frequent but mostly milestone-driven. Won-X posts. New-launch posts.
- Heavy on screenshots; light on writing-craft.

**What works:**
- Credibility chain (hackathon wins → recognition → LinkedIn views → dev tools users).
- America's audience means larger TAM than India alone.

**Mistakes:**
- Pattern reads as "achievement log" not "story." His content is findable but not memorable.
- No clear stance or contrarian opinion = no real community.

**Take:** Don't lead with achievements. Lead with a question, take, or story; let the work do the heavy proving.

## 7. Dagobert Renouf (Western founder, but adjacent)

Personal note: outside India but the Twitter-case-study video on his 0→90k journey is the cleanest case study I've seen for "founder builds Twitter audience to rescue dying startup." Key points:
- The story of *desperation* (sales = 0) is what drove the audience.
- Posting frequency rose sharply during the desperate phase, not before.
- The "founder doing his last shot" angle converts.

**Take:** Siddhant's posts should not pretend the agency is wildly profitable. He can earn more trust by sharing what isn't working yet and what he's trying next. Authenticity beats polish when you're 19 with no team.

## 9. Abhishek Veeramalla / DevOps-influencer drift

A meta-example: Abhishek V built a DevOps audience, then drifted into free-resource templates. Engagement quality dropped. The lesson: **the moment you swap "I'm building X" for "here's a free template you can use," you stop being a builder.** 

Apply to Siddhant: keep Opus and Dayo content mixed at a 70/30 ratio — 70% "what I'm building now" / 30% educational. Never reverse it.

## 10. The Bad Decisions / Varun Mayya interview pattern

Both the Varun Mayya "Bad Decisions" episode and the Singh in USA interview with Dhruv Bindra share a structure: **young founder tells the full arc in one conversation** — early wins, near-death, recovery. These *conversations* convert much better than individual takes. Siddhant doesn't need to do podcasts yet, but if any podcaster approaches, this is the format that works.

## Observed Mistake Patterns Across All Ten

These keep showing up:

1. **Achievement-led content without story** = forgettable. Reinforces the Shubham V. failure mode.
2. **Single-platform focus** = slower than expected. All the people above (Varun, Tanay, Akshay) cross-post.
3. **Pitch language creep** — once you start saying "excited to share," your growth flatlines.
4. **Hiding the failure side** — the moment you stop showing what broke, follow-count grows but DMs slow down.
5. **Trying to look bigger than you are** — premature "agency"-style copy loses the only advantage a 19-year-old has, which is audacious honesty.

The Varun Mayya approach ("I am an idiot") and the Akshay Pachaar approach (technical proof) are the two frames worth studying. Siddhant has room for both: ECE + agency + motion design = rare enough that he can copy the technical-content-practitioner lane (Akshay) but lead with the authenticity-humor cadence (Varun).

## Specific Takeaways For Siddhant

1. **Lead every post with a number, a take, or a personal failure.** Not "we did X." Not "excited to share." Not "lesson learned."
2. **72+ posts/month is unsustainable alone. ~30 posts/month max** (LinkedIn + Twitter combined) with weekends open.
3. **Mix formats:** hot-take / behind-the-scenes / number-up / contrarian-pivot. Story-based posts go 2× harder than checklist posts.
4. **Threads of 5–8 tweets** are how you break on Twitter. Single hot-takes are how you grow on LinkedIn.
5. **Behind-the-scenes wins:** screen-record analyzed work shots. Avoid making motion-design b-roll without context — without "what client + what worked," motion reels just look like everyone else's.
